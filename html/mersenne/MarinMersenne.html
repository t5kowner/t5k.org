Marin Mersenne, a short biography

Marin Mersenne was born into a family of laborers in Oizé, Maine, France 8 September 1588.  He died in Paris, France 1 September 1648.

Mersenne spent five years at the Jesuit collège at La Flèche beginning in 1604, followed by two years of theology at the Sorbonne.  In 1611, he joined the Franciscan Order of Minims, so named because they considered themselves the least of all the religious orders. Mersenne became a priest in Paris in 1612 and from 1614 to 1619, Mersenne taught philosophy at the convent at Nevers.  In 1619, Mersenne moved to the Minim Convent de l'Annonciade near Place Royal (now Place de Vosges).  Other than for a few short trips, he remained there until his death in 1648 [Lenoble1971, p. 15ff].

Mersenne greatest contribution was his continual correspondence and meetings with scientific leaders, developing an informal network for disseminating information well before the inception of scientific journals.  After Mersenne's death, letters from nearly one hundred correspondents were found in his cell.  Those that visited or corresponded with Mersenne include Descartes, Desargues, Fermat, Hobbes, Huygens, Pell, Galileo, the Pascals, and Torricelli. The collection of Mersenne's correspondence totals seventeen volumes [TW1932-88].

It was said "to inform Mersenne of a discovery, meant to publish it throughout the whole of Europe" [Source1].  He formalized his role by creating the Academia Parisiensis in 1635 [Crombie1974, p. 316], a precursor to the Académie des Sciences established in 1666 by Jean-Baptiste Colbert [Source2].  Mersennes' "personal charm made his monastery a center of life for Paris, and helped make Paris the intellectual center of Europe" [Boorstin1983].

Mersenne's early works have been described as those of a zealous polemicist.  Yet, he always advocated both science and the preservation of religion because as he wrote in his La vérité: "the true philosophy never conflicts with the belief of the Church" [Crombie1974].  His Quastiones in Genesim (1623) contained 35 arguments for the existence of God, and other defenses of orthodoxy against "atheists, magicians, deists and suchlike," but also contained a wide variety of scientific discoveries.

Mersenne published Galileo's work in French (Les Méchanique de Galilée 1634, as well parts of Galileo's Dialogo and Discorsi).  Mersenne defended Galileo and published his work, yet viewed the question of earth's motion as undecided and encouraged the search for more scientific evidence to settle the issue.  He agreed the Church should censor some opinions, but urged moderation because "the true philosophy never conflicts with the belief of the Church" [Crombie1974].

A careful experimenter insisting on precision and repetition, Mersenne was the first to discover that the frequency of a pendulum is inversely proportional to the square root of its length (published 30 June 1634 about a year before Galileo [Crombie1974, p. 319, 321]).  Mersenne also proposed the use of a pendulum as a timing device to Christiaan Huygens, inspiring him to invent the pendulum clock. Mersenne commented "one should not rely too much only on reasoning" and questioned whether Galileo actually carried out some of the experiments on acceleration down a plane that Galileo described (Harmonie Universelle, 1636-7).

Mersenne is often credited with developing the system of tuning instruments called equal temperament. However, he was preceded by the Chinese prince Chu Tsai-yü in 1596 and Galileo's father Vincenzo Galilei in 1581.  Despite the name, many think that Johann Sebastian Bach's The Well-Tempered Clavier (1722, 1744) may have been written for such a system.

Mersenne experimentally developed three important principals in the acoustics of stringed instruments (Harmonicorum Libri, 1636).  Mersenne's three laws state that that the fundamental frequency of a stretched string is

  • inversely proportional to the length of the string
  • directly proportional to the square root of the tension in the string
  • and inversely proportional to the mass per unit length of the string.

In 1936 Mersenne proposed a design for a reflecting telescope using a concave paraboloidal primary and a convex paraboloidal secondary arranged so that their focal points coincide (see, for example [Bone1999], [Linfoot1955, chapter 4], [Arrioti1977]). The Electro-Optics Branch at NASA's Marshall Space Flight Center uses an off axis Mersenne telescope in a lidar (light detection and ranging) system [Bone1999].

Mersenne may be currently best known for his guess on which integers of the form 2n-1 (now called Mersenne numbers) are actually prime. In the preface to his Cogitata Physica-Mathematica (1644) that the numbers 2n-1 were prime for

n = 2, 3, 5, 7, 13, 17, 19, 31, 67, 127 and 257

and were composite for all other positive integers n < 257. At the time it was known that these numbers were primes for n = 2, 3, 5, 7, 13, 17, and 19.

It was not until 1947 that Mersenne's range, n < 257, had been completely checked and it was determined that the correct list is:

n = 2, 3, 5, 7, 13, 17, 19, 31, 61, 89, 107 and 127.

Now approximately 40 Mersenne primes are known and the have millions of digits.

Mersenne published results on the cycloid, reported on the chemistry of tin and discussed a "sensitive plant" from the West Indies [Boorstin1983].  Mersenne sought a perfect language that was natural and universal for communication of scientific ideas.
